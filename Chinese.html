Chinese Food History


Chinese food revolved around gastronomy; tasting, experiencing, and seeing the food, not just eating. By the time confucius came along, food had practically become an art. If the rice was not white, or if the meat was too thin, people would not eat it. Cooking was a serious business, and an inexperienced chef could easily make mistakes, leading to bad sales, and ultimately, a close of business. Due to the adoption of cutlery, the Chinese made foods bite sized pieces, or tender enough to be picked apart easily, for convenience when using chopsticks. During the han dynasty, the Chinese started to work on forms of preservation, such as jerky, in order to boost the lifetime of the supplies the soldiers needed to survive. Finally, during the qing dynasty, gastronomy started to make a comeback, and famous gastronomes such as Yuan Mei started to focus primarily on extracting the maximum taste, and flavour of the food.

Chinese Food now

Now, fast food such as deep fried noodles and rice are starting to make an appearance. Basic etiquette is that youths should not sit down before elders, and similarly, youths should not eat before elders. Holding the bottom part of a bowl is looked down upon as well, since it resembles the act of begging, and putting chopsticks in a bowl of rice vertically isn’t accepted either, since it resembles the tradition of putting a bowl of rice over the dead. Knives are also considered not respectful, as chopsticks can do the job. Chinese food now still revolves around it’s history; most meals are bite sized or tender, and gastronomy is still a huge part of the chinese culture. In fact, you can still see this today. For example, many Chinese favourites are bite sized, such as spring rolls, dumplings, and all types of asian meat. They are also quite visually pleasing, and in order to keep the flavours to the maximum, Asians even developed MSG, a "drug" that keeps flavours to a max, and it's widely used in cheap food in order to enhance the taste. It's also used as last resort to try to keep cheap food on par with traditional food.
